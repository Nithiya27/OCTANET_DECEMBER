"A book is the only place in which you can examine a fragile thought without breaking it, or explore an explosive idea without fear it will go off in your face. It is one of the few havens remaining where a man’s mind can get both provocation and privacy." - Edward P. Morgan

"The Psychology of Money" by Morgan Housel is a book that explores the emotional and psychological aspects of financial decision-making. It delves into the impact of human behavior, cognitive biases, and emotions on money-related choices. The author emphasizes the importance of concepts like saving, understanding risk, having a long-term perspective, and adapting to change. The book encourages readers to rethink their approach to wealth and offers practical lessons for achieving financial success.

"Rich Dad Poor Dad" by Robert T. Kiyosaki contrasts the financial philosophies of the author's two father figures—his biological father (Poor Dad) and the father of his childhood friend (Rich Dad). The book emphasizes the importance of a mindset geared towards financial education, asset-building, and entrepreneurship. Kiyosaki advocates for understanding the difference between assets and liabilities, breaking free from the "rat race," and working to learn rather than solely for money.

"Think Like a Monk" by Jay Shetty is a self-help book that draws on the author's experiences as a former monk to provide practical insights for finding purpose and happiness. It explores mindfulness, purposeful living, positive habits, building meaningful relationships, overcoming negativity, and the transformative power of service. The book serves as a guide to adopting a monk-like mindset for a more fulfilling life.

"Atomic Habits" by James Clear explores the transformative power of small habits. The book emphasizes the compounding effect of tiny changes over time and introduces practical strategies such as habit stacking, the two-minute rule, and identity-based habits. Clear underscores the significance of environment and continuous improvement, advocating for a focus on systems rather than goals. Overall, the book provides a practical framework for creating positive habits and achieving long-term success.

"Mindset: The New Psychology of Success" by Carol S. Dweck explores the concepts of fixed and growth mindsets. Dweck argues that individuals with a fixed mindset believe abilities are innate and unchangeable, while those with a growth mindset see potential for development through effort. The book illustrates how mindsets impact success, resilience, learning from failure, parenting, teaching, and corporate culture. Dweck encourages readers to embrace a growth mindset for continuous learning, development, and achievement.

"The Secret" by Rhonda Byrne promotes the Law of Attraction, suggesting that positive thoughts attract positive outcomes. Emphasizing the power of positive thinking, visualization, and gratitude, the book encourages readers to focus on desired goals and maintain belief in their achievement. While advocating for these principles, "The Secret" has garnered both praise and criticism for its approach to success and manifestation.

"Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind" by Yuval Noah Harari is a sweeping exploration of human history. Harari covers key milestones such as the Cognitive Revolution, Agricultural Revolution, and Scientific Revolution, examining their impact on society. The book explores the unification of humankind through empires and trade, the rise of capitalism and imperialism, and the challenges of the modern era. It concludes by contemplating the potential consequences of the ongoing biotechnological revolution. "Sapiens" provides a thought-provoking overview of the forces that have shaped human civilization.

APJ Abdul Kalam's autobiography, Wings of Fire, covers his early life as well as his role in Indian space research and missile programmes. It tells the narrative of a young boy from a poor family who rose through the ranks of Indian space research and missile programmes to become the country's president.
